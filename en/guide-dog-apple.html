Can Dogs Eat Apples?

Let's start with the answer: yes, they can. Just be sure to remove the seeds and core, and keep the portion in check.

Apples are a great treat for dogs

Apples are one of the classic safe fruits that dogs can eat. They're rich in vitamins A and C and dietary fiber, and low in fat and protein, so they're easy on senior dogs and dogs on a diet. At roughly 52–57 kcal per 100 g they're fairly low in calories, and because many dogs love the crunchy texture, apples work well as a training treat too. Chewing on apple also has the side benefit of helping to clean the surface of the teeth.

Why are the seeds and core dangerous?

Apple seeds contain a compound called amygdalin, which turns into cyanide in the body when the seeds are chewed and crushed. Poisoning from just a few seeds is rare, but eating them repeatedly, or a small dog chewing several of them, can be risky — so the rule is to always remove every seed. As for the hard core, the bigger concern isn't toxicity but the risk of it getting stuck in the throat or blocking the intestines. Don't give your dog a whole apple; cut out the core and seeds and serve only the flesh in bite-sized pieces.

How much can you give?

The rule of thumb is that treats should not exceed 10% of your dog's daily calorie needs. For apples, here are rough daily limits by body weight.

  • Small dogs (around 5 kg): 30–50 g per day (2–4 thin slices)
  • Medium dogs (10–15 kg): 70–100 g per day (about a quarter of an apple)
  • Large dogs (25 kg and up): 150–200 g per day (about half an apple)

If you're curious about your dog's daily calorie needs, check the Dog Food Portion Calculator. Ten percent of the daily calories it gives you is your treat limit. If it's your dog's first time, it's safest to start with one or two slices and watch for diarrhea or vomiting over the next day or so.

Be careful in these cases

Apples contain fructose, so for dogs with diabetes it's safest to feed them only after consulting your vet. The same goes for dogs on a prescription diet for kidney or digestive conditions. If you serve the apple with the peel on, wash off pesticide residue and wax thoroughly under running water, and for dogs with weak digestion, peel it first. Apple juice, apple jam, and pie made for people are high in sugar and additives, so it's best not to give them. On days you offer a treat, it's a good idea to keep an eye on water intake too with the Daily Water Intake Calculator.

My dog swallowed a few apple seeds. Is this an emergency?

It's rare for a dog to show poisoning symptoms just from swallowing a few seeds. The toxic compound in the seeds is released only when they are chewed and crushed — swallowed whole, they usually pass right through. That said, if you notice vomiting, drooling, breathing problems, or lethargy, go to the vet right away. If a small dog swallowed the whole core, there is a risk of intestinal blockage, so keep an eye on their stools for a few days.

Can I give my dog the apple peel? What about dried apple chips?

The peel is high in fiber, so a small amount is fine, but it may carry pesticide residue and wax, so wash it thoroughly. For dogs with sensitive digestion, it's safer to peel the apple and give only the flesh. Dried apple chips and apple juice made for people have concentrated sugar and often contain additives, so we don't recommend them. Cutting fresh flesh into small pieces is the best option.
