How to Choose a Cat Scratcher

You can't stop the scratching — it's an essential behavior. But you can decide where they scratch.

Why Do Cats Scratch?

Scratching is an essential feline behavior: it sheds the worn outer sheath of the claws, marks territory with the scent glands in the paw pads, and stretches out the muscles like a good yawn. Scolding your cat for it only builds up stress and adds more secret scratching spots. There's only one strategy — put a scratcher more appealing than the sofa in a spot better than the sofa.

Vertical vs. Horizontal: Figure Out the Preference First

  • The vertical type — if your cat scratches the wallpaper, the side of the sofa, or door frames: go with a post-style or wall-mounted scratcher. It should be taller than your cat when fully stretched (at least 60 cm / 24 in, and sturdy enough not to wobble).
  • The horizontal type — if your cat scratches the carpet, mats, or the floor: go with a flat pad-style or bed-style scratcher that sits on the ground.
  • Not sure? Start with a corrugated cardboard pad — it's affordable, so there's little to lose if it doesn't work out.

Material Types

Cardboard is cheap and cats tend to love it, but it sheds dust and doesn't last long. Sisal rope is highly durable and the go-to standard for post-style scratchers, while carpet fabric can backfire — your cat may confuse it with your home carpet and actually reinforce carpet-scratching, so use it with care. The answer is to try several materials and find the one your cat keeps coming back to.

Placement Is Half the Battle

A scratcher gets used when it's on your cat's walking path, not tucked into a corner. The best spots are near their sleeping area (for the wake-up stretch-and-scratch combo) and right next to the sofa they're already scratching. If you want to protect your sofa, the standard three-step approach is: place a scratcher next to the sofa → sprinkle catnip on the scratcher to draw them in → temporarily cover the sofa to make it less appealing. Once the habit sticks, you can gradually move the scratcher to where you want it.

I bought a scratcher but my cat won't even look at it.

Chances are one of three things is off: the location (shoved into a corner), the stability (it wobbles), or the material preference. Move it onto your cat's walking path, sprinkle some catnip or silvervine on it, and try again. If your cat still ignores it, the fastest fix is to switch to a product with a different material or orientation.

Does trimming the claws reduce scratching?

It won't reduce how often they scratch, but it will reduce furniture damage. Scratching is an instinctive behavior that goes beyond claw maintenance, so even with trimmed claws your cat still needs a place to scratch. The right approach is to do both together.
