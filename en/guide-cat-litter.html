How to Choose Cat Litter

The bottom line first: there is no single "best" litter — the best litter is the one your cat actually uses. Judge by your cat's standards (texture, unscented) before your own (odor, cleanup).

The four main types — pros and cons

Cats use the litter box several times a day, dig and cover with their paws, and then groom themselves. That makes litter a hygiene and health product, not just a consumable. Products on the market fall into four main types.

  • Bentonite (clumping clay) — urine forms firm clumps that are easy to scoop, and the fine grains feel closest to natural sand, so cats tend to prefer it. The downsides: some products are dusty, and fine grains stick to paws and scatter around the box (tracking). Never flush it — it hardens and clogs pipes, so dispose of it in the trash.
  • Tofu litter (soy-based) — low-dust and lightweight, and many products can be flushed in small amounts, which makes cleanup easy. Being plant-based, it's less of a worry if swallowed. The trade-offs: clumping is weaker than bentonite, and in humid summer weather it needs airtight storage so it doesn't absorb moisture or attract insects.
  • Crystal (silica gel) — absorbs moisture and odor very well, so odor control is strong and it lasts longer between changes. However, urine doesn't clump, which makes it hard to monitor how much your cat is peeing, and some cats dislike the coarse, hard texture.
  • Wood / paper pellets — low dust, low tracking, and eco-friendly. Many don't clump and are used with a dedicated sifting litter box, and texture preferences vary from cat to cat.

A 5-point checklist before you buy

  • ① Clumping power — firm clumps mean less wasted litter and a cleaner box. For clumping types, the key is whether clumps scoop out whole without crumbling.
  • ② Dust — dust raised when pouring or digging is bad for both feline and human airways. Look for a "low-dust" label, and if your cat has a sensitive respiratory system, prioritize low-dust types (tofu, crystal, pellets).
  • ③ Scent — a cat's sense of smell is far more sensitive than ours. Strongly scented litter is a common cause of box avoidance, so unscented is the safe default.
  • ④ Tracking — the finer the grain, the more it scatters. If tracking drives you crazy, consider coarser-grain types or add a litter-trapping mat.
  • ⑤ Disposal — bentonite must never go down the toilet; even for tofu litter, check the "flushable" label and your plumbing, and flush only small amounts. Whether a type fits your home's disposal setup matters more than you'd think.

More important than the litter itself — litter box basics

No litter can compensate for poor box management. The basics: one box per cat plus one extra, litter about 5–7 cm (2–3 in) deep, and clumps scooped once or twice a day. In humid summer weather, odor and moisture build up faster, so move up your full-change and box-washing schedule. One big advantage of clumping litter is that clump size and count let you check urination daily — clumps that suddenly shrink or multiply can signal cystitis or kidney problems. Can I use clumping bentonite litter for a kitten?

Kittens under about 2–3 months old often lick or eat litter out of curiosity. Bentonite hardens when it meets moisture, so swallowing a lot of it can burden a kitten's digestive tract. It's safer to start young kittens on tofu or paper litter, which is less risky if swallowed, and switch to bentonite once the litter-eating behavior stops. If your kitten shows vomiting, lethargy, or abnormal stool after eating litter, contact a vet.

I switched litters and now my cat won't use the box. Why?

Cats are extremely sensitive to paw texture and smell, so replacing all the litter at once can make them reject the box entirely. Start by mixing only 20–30% of the new litter into the old one, then raise the ratio gradually over one to two weeks. If your cat keeps holding it in, or you see urination outside the box or blood in the urine, it could be a health issue such as cystitis — see a vet.
