Heatstroke in Cats: Symptoms and Emergency First Aid

The bottom line first: if your cat is panting with its mouth open, that's already a warning sign. Move them somewhere cool, wet them down with lukewarm water, and contact a vet right away.

Yes, cats get heatstroke — even indoors

It's easy to assume cats handle heat well, but they can't sweat over their whole body the way humans do, so their options for regulating temperature are very limited. A little sweat through the paw pads and grooming are about all they have. That means heatstroke can strike even indoors — in a sealed room in midsummer, in direct sunlight by a window, or inside a carrier with no airflow. A cat's normal body temperature is roughly 38.1–39.2°C (100.5–102.5°F); above 40°C (104°F) you should suspect heatstroke, and above 41°C (106°F) it's an emergency where organ damage can begin.

Symptoms to watch for

  • Open-mouth breathing (panting) — in a cat, this alone is an abnormal sign.
  • Drooling, gums or tongue turning deep red
  • Lethargy, stumbling, slow response when called
  • Vomiting or diarrhea, and in severe cases seizures or loss of consciousness

Flat-faced breeds like Persians and Exotic Shorthairs, overweight cats, senior cats and kittens, and cats with heart or respiratory disease are at much higher risk, so they need extra care in summer.

Emergency first aid at home: 4 steps

  • 1) Move them somewhere cool immediately — an air-conditioned room or a shaded, well-ventilated spot.
  • 2) Wet the body with lukewarm water — dampen a towel with lukewarm water and wipe the torso, armpits, and groin, and use a fan on a low setting. No ice water or alcohol.
  • 3) Offer drinking water — if your cat can drink on its own, provide fresh water, but never force water into its mouth.
  • 4) Get to a vet right away — even if your cat seems to recover, organ damage can show up hours later, so always have them examined. Keep the wet towel and airflow going during the ride.

Prevention is the best medicine in summer

When you go out in midsummer, use air conditioning, a fan, or ventilation to keep the room from overheating, and block direct sunlight with curtains. Placing fresh water bowls in several spots around the home also helps with hydration. Never leave a cat alone in a car, even for a few minutes. My cat is breathing with its mouth open. Is this an emergency?

Unlike dogs, cats don't normally pant with their mouths open. If your cat is open-mouth breathing and hasn't just finished an intense play session, it can be a sign of heatstroke or a heart or respiratory problem — move them somewhere cool and contact a vet right away.

Can I cool my cat down quickly with ice water or ice packs?

No. Anything ice-cold constricts the blood vessels in the skin, which actually traps heat inside, and dropping the body temperature too fast can cause hypothermia. The safe approach is to wet the body with lukewarm water and cool gradually with airflow.
