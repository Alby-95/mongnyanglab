Grapes and Dogs: Never Give Them, Ever

Unlike watermelon, grapes are a no-exceptions forbidden food. Raisins are even more dangerous.

Why are they dangerous?

Grapes and raisins can cause acute kidney failure (a shutdown of kidney function) in dogs. What makes it so frightening is that the exact toxic compound and the dangerous dose still haven't been identified — some dogs show severe poisoning after just a few, while others seem perfectly fine, and that mix means "my dog will be okay" simply doesn't hold. That's why the veterinary community's shared guidance is a single rule: never feed them, no matter the amount.

Because raisins have lost their water content and their compounds are concentrated, they are more dangerous than grapes by weight. The same goes for raisins baked into bread or cereal.

If you catch your dog eating them

  • Contact a vet right away, no matter the amount. The rule is to get instructions from the clinic even for just one or two
  • Within 2 hours of ingestion, the clinic can induce vomiting — this is the golden window
  • Don't force your dog to vomit at home. Done wrong, it risks aspiration pneumonia
  • Check the packaging or what's left over so you know how many were eaten

Signs of poisoning

Within a few hours to 24 hours of ingestion, you may see vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, and loss of appetite, and in severe cases it can progress to reduced urine output (a sign of kidney failure). Even without symptoms, you can't relax — kidney damage shows up on the outside only later, so if you're sure your dog ate them, it's safest to see a vet whether or not symptoms are present.

Other forbidden foods to watch out for besides grapes

The main ones are chocolate (theobromine), xylitol (sugar-free gum and candy — even a small amount is an emergency), onions and garlic (anemia), macadamia nuts, and alcohol. Chocolate's risk level varies with how much was eaten, so check it right away with the chocolate toxicity calculator.

Are grape juice and grape skins off-limits too?

Yes, they are. Avoid grapes in every form — juice, skins, jam, and even bread made with raisins. The danger has been reported regardless of whether the grapes are processed.

My dog ate some before and was fine. Doesn't that mean mine isn't at risk?

No. Researchers generally agree that grape toxicity can affect even the same dog differently from one time to the next. Being fine once is no guarantee it will be safe the next time.
